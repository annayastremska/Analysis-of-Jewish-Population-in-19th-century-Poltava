Data Analysis · Historical Demography

Jewish Population Density in Poltava Governorate

Their role in society and relationship with other groups in the 19th century Russian Empire

Author Anna Yastremska
Dataset Poltava Governorate Population, 19th Century
Period 1835 – 1850
Region Pale of Settlement, Russian Empire

The Poltava Governorate was part of the Pale of Settlement — the western regions of the Russian Empire where Jews were permitted to reside. This analysis examines where Jewish communities concentrated, how they related to commercial classes, and whether the presence of Cossacks affected Jewish settlement patterns.

from scipy import stats

def remove_outliers_iqr(df, column):
    Q1, Q3 = df[column].quantile(0.25), df[column].quantile(0.75)
    IQR = Q3 - Q1
    return df[(df[column] >= Q1 - 1.5 * IQR) & (df[column] <= Q3 + 1.5 * IQR)]

Conclusion

The correlation between Jews and merchants exists only in major commercial centers. Once outliers are removed, it vanishes completely — revealing that the relationship is not structural across the governorate. The correlation with townspeople is positive but not statistically significant.

Imperial restrictions barred Jews from agriculture by prohibiting land ownership. Consequently, they turned to trade and crafts. The data confirms Jews succeeded in commercial activities within authorized urban settlements, while remaining largely absent from rural economies.

Task 03

The Cossack Question — More Cossacks, Fewer Jews?

Hypothesis Cossack regions were predominantly agrarian and had frequent conflicts with Jews — predicting a negative correlation

Drawing on 1850 population data, this analysis tests whether districts with large Cossack populations had correspondingly smaller Jewish populations. Outliers are removed and a Pearson correlation is calculated to determine statistical significance.

Conclusion

The correlation between the number of Cossacks and Jews is non-existent. The hypothesis was proved to be false.

Jews concentrated in commercial towns while Cossacks remained in military settlements and agricultural areas — suggesting spatial separation rather than active conflict. This also reflects the legacy of 17th-century violence that established patterns of mutual avoidance.

Summary of Findings

01
Кременчуцький district emerged as the dominant Jewish center, with the highest concentration throughout the period. The growth of the Jewish population in Kremenchuk is linked to the city's economic expansion — Jews from small towns moved there in search of work, as it was a major regional trading hub.
02
Merchants showed a strong correlation with Jewish population, but only in major commercial centers — not rural districts. Imperial restrictions barred Jews from agriculture, forcing them toward trade and crafts. Jewish economic activity was highly concentrated in specific urban centers, absent from rural economies.
03
